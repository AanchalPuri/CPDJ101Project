What is a Deepfake?

Deepfakes are AI-generated media (images, videos, or audio) that are designed to look and sound like real people. Using machine learning, anyone can now fabricate a person saying or doing something they never did. What used to require a Hollywood studio can now be done with a free app in minutes.

In the News

🚫 Non-Consensual Images

One of the most damaging uses of deepfakes is creating explicit images of real people without their consent. 96% of deepfakes online target women, especially including minors. Victims face harassment, damaged reputations, and lasting psychological harm, often with no legal recourse.

💸 The Scam Revolution

Scammers use deepfakes to impersonate celebrities endorsing fake products, run romance scams with fabricated photos, and even clone voices of family members to demand emergency money transfers. Because the content looks and sounds real, victims have no reason to doubt it until it's too late.

🗳️ Election Interference

In January 2024, AI-cloned robocalls mimicking President Biden's voice told New Hampshire voters to skip the Democratic primary. Deepfakes are now a direct threat to democratic participation and the technology to create them is free and widely available.

Spot the Signs

Unnatural Blinking

Early deepfakes rarely blinked. Newer ones blink too perfectly or at wrong/unatural moments.

Mismatched Lighting

Shadows on the face don't match the background or the setting the "person" is in. Look for inconsistent light direction.

Weird Hair & Face edges

Hair strands, glasses, and face edges often look blurry, wavy, or unnaturally smooth.

Strange Teeth

AI struggles with individual teeth. Look for a blurred, merged, or overly perfect set.

Ear & Jewelry Issues

Earrings, piercings, and ear shapes are commonly distorted or asymmetrical in deepfakes.

Flat Emotions

Facial expressions may not match the tone of speech.

POLICY-LEVEL SOLUTIONS

Deepfakes are a shared problem that requires system-wide responses. Here are the three main policy approaches being pursued to reduce harm.

Legislation

What it does: Laws create legal consequences for deepfake abuse and give victims a path to justice. The DEFIANCE Act (2024) provides civil remedies for non-consensual synthetic images. Proposed laws like the NO FAKES Act aim to protect voice and likeness from unauthorized use.

The gap: Most laws are reactive and help after harm is done, but not before. Laws also vary by state and country, leaving some people protected and others vulnerable. And enforcement is slow; by the time a case moves through courts, misinformation has already spread widely.

What matters: Legislation signals that synthetic media abuse is serious, but it works best when paired with faster detection and platform intervention.

Platform Moderation

What it does: Platforms can label, remove, or de-amplify deepfakes, slowing their spread before they reach millions. Some platforms use automation to flag potential synthetic media, then humans review flagged content.

The gap: Moderation is slow and inconsistent. A viral deepfake can reach thousands of people before any human sees it. Rules also vary by platform, and bad actors simply repost content across multiple sites or private channels where moderation is weaker.

What matters: Platform policies set the baseline for what users see. If platforms deprioritize deepfakes without banning them outright, they control the spread before lawmakers or courts get involved.

Model Guardrails

What it does: Guardrails built into AI models can refuse harmful requests, add watermarks to synthetic media, or limit the generation of explicit content. This aims to stop deepfakes at the source before they are ever created.

The gap: Guardrails only work on the tools that implement them. Open-source models, local installations, and third-party apps bypass built-in safety features. A motivated creator can find tools without guardrails, rendering this approach incomplete.

What matters: Strong guardrails reduce casual misuse and raise the barrier to entry for bad actors. Combined with other policies, they make it harder (but not impossible) to create harmful synthetic media.

The Bottom Line: None of these three approaches works alone. Legislation without platform action lets viral deepfakes spread unchecked. Platform moderation without legislation leaves creators unpunished. Guardrails without enforcement get bypassed. The best outcomes happen when all three are strong and coordinated.

What Can You Do?

Deepfakes are a shared problem. Here's how you can protect yourself, report harm, and stay informed.

Protect Yourself

  • Limit public photos on social media
  • Use privacy settings to restrict who sees your images
  • Reverse image search suspicious photos (Google Images, TinEye)
  • Be skeptical of urgent video/voice messages

Spot & Verify

  • Use deepware.ai to scan suspicious videos
  • Check Reuters Fact Check and Snopes for viral content
  • Look for blurry edges, weird teeth, or unnatural blinking
  • Slow down videos to spot frame glitches

Report It

  • Report deepfakes on platforms using their content violation tools
  • Contact the FBI's IC3 for deepfake fraud
  • Use the StopNCII tool for non-consensual intimate images
  • Document everything (screenshots, URLs, timestamps)

Know the Laws

  • DEFIANCE Act (2024) = civil remedies for non-consensual deepfake victims in the US
  • NO FAKES Act proposed federal protection of voice and likeness
  • Many states have their own deepfake laws, so check your state legislature
  • congress.gov — track current legislation
